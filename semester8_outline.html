Day 1
Design Brief ‚Äî Translating HMW Into a Buildable Specification
Intro
Students arrive with their Semester 7 HMW statements. Today's work is writing a Design Brief ‚Äî a 1-page document that translates the HMW into a buildable specification. A Design Brief has five sections: (1) Problem Statement ‚Äî who is this for and what is the core need; (2) Constraints ‚Äî what limits must the design respect (size, budget ~$15, materials available in the makerspace, timeline); (3) Success Criteria ‚Äî how will you know if the design works? List at least 3 measurable criteria; (4) Technical Layer ‚Äî what domains will this project require (CAD only? Electronics? Code? Python analysis? All of the above?); (5) Out of Scope ‚Äî what would be in v2 but is explicitly not in this semester's build. The "Out of Scope" section is the most important ‚Äî it forces students to cut before they start. Teacher reviews all briefs before Day 2.

Day 2
Project Planning ‚Äî Gantt Chart, Milestones & Contingency
Skill
Students build their first Gantt chart ‚Äî a timeline showing which tasks happen in which weeks, with explicit milestone markers (lo-fi test, CAD print submission, electronics working, report draft). Teacher introduces the concept of contingency: every project plan should include at least one buffer period, because something always takes longer than expected. Students identify the single riskiest task in their project (the one most likely to blow the schedule) and note it explicitly. Gantt is built on paper or in a simple spreadsheet ‚Äî not complex software. The lesson closes with a peer review: swap plans with a partner and give one piece of feedback on scope or timeline realism.

Design Review Gate #1 ‚Äî Teacher Brief Review + Scope Adjustment
Gate
Each student has a 5-minute individual design review with the teacher. The teacher evaluates: Is the project feasible in 20 class days? Is the technical complexity appropriate (not too simple, not impossibly ambitious)? Is the scope specific enough to deliver a testable prototype? Students who pass the review receive a "green light" notation in their notebook. Students who need revision receive written teacher notes and have until the start of Day 4 to revise. No student begins ideation or CAD until they have a green light. This gate mirrors professional project approval ‚Äî work does not proceed to execution without review and sign-off.

Days 6‚Äì7
Peer Design Critique ‚Äî Gallery Walk & Structured Feedback
Skill
Before committing to CAD, every student participates in a structured peer design critique ‚Äî the most rigorous feedback session in the program so far. Each student posts their lo-fi prototype, design brief, and concept selection rationale at a station. During a 20-minute gallery walk, every classmate visits every station and leaves sticky-note feedback using the "I Like / I Wonder / What If" framework: "I Like" (something genuinely strong), "I Wonder" (a question about feasibility, user need, or scope), "What If" (a specific alternative idea). Each student returns to their station, reads all feedback, and writes a 5-sentence "Critique Response" ‚Äî what they are keeping, what they are changing, and what feedback they are consciously rejecting (and why). Rejecting feedback with a clear reason is as valid as accepting it.

Days 14‚Äì15
User Test Against Success Criteria ‚Äî The Hardest Feedback
Assess
The most important two days of the semester. Each student conducts a structured user test with a person from (or closely representative of) their original target user group ‚Äî ideally someone from the Semester 7 multi-person study. The test protocol uses the 3-part framework from Semester 6, now extended: (1) silent observation ‚Äî give the prototype without explanation and watch; (2) structured task ‚Äî ask them to accomplish one specific thing using the prototype; (3) success criteria evaluation ‚Äî for each of the 3+ measurable success criteria from the brief, evaluate pass/fail and document why. The User Test Report is graded on rigor and honesty. A prototype that fails 2 of 3 criteria but has rigorous documentation will score higher than one that passes all criteria but has vague notes.

Day 15 (cont.)
Iteration Sprint ‚Äî Prioritize, Fix, Document v2
Studio
Immediately following the user test, students triage findings using a priority matrix: impact (how much does this affect the user's experience?) vs. effort (how long will this take to fix?). High impact, low effort changes are done today. High impact, high effort changes are noted as "v2 priority." Low impact changes are noted but deprioritized. The v2 section of the notebook becomes a living document of everything the student knows would improve the design but did not have time to implement. This is standard professional practice ‚Äî all shipped products have a v2 backlog ‚Äî and normalizes honest scope management.

Year 4 Final Showcase ‚Äî Person ¬∑ Process ¬∑ Data ¬∑ Product
Assess
The Year 4 Final Showcase uses an expanded four-beat format that previews the Sophomore capstone presentation structure: (1) The Person ‚Äî who did you design for, what research did you do, what did you learn that surprised you? (2) The Process ‚Äî one key decision at each stage of the project (ideation, critique, CAD, electronics/code, testing); (3) The Data ‚Äî show the Python analysis chart or user test results ‚Äî quantitative evidence about your design; (4) The Product ‚Äî live demo. Invited audience includes other teachers, parents, and ideally at least one of the people interviewed in the Semester 7 research study. 5 minutes per student. Teacher and peer rubrics both completed. This is the closest the program has come to a professional engineering presentation ‚Äî and it is explicitly named as the capstone preview. Students are told: "The Sophomore capstone will look like this, with more time, a bigger audience, and work you've been building toward for four years."
