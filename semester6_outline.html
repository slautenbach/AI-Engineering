Intro

Day 2
SCAMPER & Crazy 8s ‚Äî Generating Ideas Quickly and Widely
Skill
Students apply two rapid ideation techniques to their own HMW statement. First: SCAMPER (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Rearrange) ‚Äî a systematic lens for generating variations on existing approaches. Second: Crazy 8s ‚Äî 8 rough sketches in 8 minutes, forcing volume over quality. Key lesson: in ideation, quantity comes first; evaluation comes later. Students produce at least 12 distinct concepts. All sketches are taped into their notebooks ‚Äî no erasing, no discarding.

Day 3
Morphological Chart ‚Äî Systematic Concept Combination
Skill
Students build a morphological chart: a grid where each row is a sub-function of their design (e.g., "how it attaches," "how it alerts the user," "what material it uses") and each column is a different option for that sub-function. Drawing one path through the grid ‚Äî one option per row ‚Äî generates a unique concept combination. Students build charts with at least 4 rows and 3 options each, then identify 3 promising combinations to develop further. Notebook entry is the chart itself with 3 paths circled and labeled.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 3.3 IAS-Math 9-10.A

Day 4
Concept Screening Matrix ‚Äî Selecting One Concept to Build
Skill
Students evaluate their 3 concept combinations using a Concept Screening Matrix (Pugh Matrix): rows are weighted criteria (addresses user need, feasibility, cost, complexity, novelty), columns are concepts. Each cell gets +, 0, or ‚Äì relative to a reference. The concept with the most net positives is the recommended choice. Teacher emphasizes the matrix is a thinking tool, not a rule ‚Äî students must write a "Concept Selection Rationale" paragraph defending their choice, including any case where they deviate from the matrix result.

CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 3.3 CTE-TE 4.1
CTE-TE 4.1 (technical communication) is fulfilled through the written Concept Selection Rationale ‚Äî students must translate a matrix result into a clear, reasoned argument. This mirrors the engineering decision memos used in industry design reviews.

Day 5
Low-Fidelity Prototype ‚Äî Paper, Cardboard & Tape First
Skill
Before any CAD or code, students build a lo-fi prototype of their chosen concept using paper, cardboard, foam, tape, and markers. The prototype must communicate form and function clearly enough that an unfamiliar observer can understand it. Students bring it to a partner and ask them to "use" it ‚Äî observing where they hesitate or misunderstand. This is their first user test. Observations go directly into the notebook as "lo-fi test findings," which must inform the CAD model in Unit 2.

Day 6
CAD Planning Day ‚Äî No Computers Until the Plan Is Approved
Project
Students do not open CAD software today. Instead they plan: sketching orthographic views of the intended model in their notebook, listing each part, identifying required SolidWorks features, estimating critical dimensions, and documenting how lo-fi test findings changed the design. The planning document is reviewed by the teacher before modeling begins. Students who attempt to skip planning and go straight to CAD are redirected. This gate mirrors a professional design review ‚Äî work does not proceed to the next phase without sign-off.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2
The planning document is a pre-CAD technical drawing (CTE-TE 5.1 and 5.2) ‚Äî students must communicate geometry clearly enough for a reviewer to assess feasibility before any digital work begins. This is the industry practice of a design review gate.

Day 13
User Test #2 ‚Äî Testing the Real Prototype With a Real Person
Assess
Students conduct a structured user test of their working prototype with a classmate not involved in their design process. The 3-part protocol: (1) hand the tester the device without explanation and observe; (2) ask 3 open-ended questions about their experience; (3) document findings in a User Test Report. Graded formally ‚Äî not on whether the prototype works perfectly, but on the quality and honesty of the test and documentation. Students with broken prototypes still conduct the test and document what broke and why. Honest failure documentation scores higher than concealed failure.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.4 CTE-TE 8.1 CTE-TE 8.2 CTE-TE 9.1 IAS-ELA 9-10.W
The emphasis on process quality over product perfection is a deliberate pedagogical signal ‚Äî grading honest documentation of failure higher than concealed failure teaches students that iteration is the expected outcome of testing, not a sign of poor design.
User Test Report Rubric (Formal)

3 open-ended questions asked

Days 14‚Äì15
Iteration Sprint ‚Äî Refine Based on Test Findings
Studio
Students triage Day 13 user test findings: what can be fixed in the time remaining vs. what becomes a documented v2 improvement? Students prioritize the single most impactful change and implement it ‚Äî whether a CAD revision and reprint, a code fix, a wiring adjustment, or a UX change. All changes are documented in the notebook with "before" and "after" states. The iteration sprint reinforces the core lesson: the goal of testing is always learning that leads to change, never validation of what already exists.
