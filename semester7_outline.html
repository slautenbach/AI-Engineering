Day 1
Python vs JavaScript ‚Äî Same Logic, Different Dialect
Intro
Teacher opens with identical programs written in both JavaScript and Python ‚Äî a volume calculator function, a for loop printing a table, an if/else classifier. Students identify what is the same (logic, structure, variable concept, function concept) and what differs (indentation vs braces, print vs console.log, no semicolons, snake_case vs camelCase). Key message: you already know how to program. You are learning a new dialect. Students set up Python (IDLE, VS Code, or browser-based repl.it) and write their first Python lines interactively in the shell.
Indiana Standards
IAS-CS 9-10.AP.2 IAS-CS 9-10.AP.1
IAS-CS 9-10.AP.2 (NEW): Create clearly named variables, define functions, and use data structures to store and manipulate data. Python's readable syntax makes this standard especially concrete ‚Äî naming conventions and structure are language-enforced.

Day 3
File I/O and CSV ‚Äî Reading and Writing Real Data
Skill
Students use Python's built-in csv module to read the sensor log CSV files they produced in Semester 5 ‚Äî putting their own real data to work in a new language. Day 5
AI-Assisted Python ‚Äî Fact-Checking in an Unfamiliar Language
Skill
Students revisit the 5-step AI fact-checking framework in the Python context ‚Äî noting what transfers (logic tracing, edge case testing, run-and-verify) and what requires extra vigilance (Python idioms students don't yet recognize: generators, context managers, walrus operators). They apply the framework to 3 AI-generated Python snippets of increasing complexity. One intentional error is a Python-specific idiom that works differently than its JS equivalent ‚Äî students must catch it. The lesson reinforces that the verification mindset is language-agnostic and that unfamiliarity with a language demands more rigor, not less.

Day 7
Communication Protocols ‚Äî Why Devices Need Rules to Share a Bus
Intro
Students learn why communication protocols exist: multiple sensors sharing a single connection need rules for taking turns and identifying themselves. Teacher introduces I¬≤C (two-wire, 7-bit device addresses, multi-device bus), SPI (faster, dedicated chip-select), and UART (the Serial port they already know, now understood at the protocol level). Students focus on I¬≤C: they wire an I¬≤C OLED display or LCD and run an I¬≤C Scanner sketch to discover device addresses on the bus. The scanner is their first hardware debugging experience at the protocol level ‚Äî "the address 0x3C is present" is both a result and a diagnostic tool.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 7.1 CTE-TE 7.2 IAS-Sci 9-10.PS
CTE-TE 7.2 (NEW): Analyze and apply digital communication systems in engineering designs. I¬≤C, SPI, and UART are the three foundational standards ‚Äî this new standard anchors all multi-device electronics work from here forward through the capstone.

Day 8
I¬≤C Sensor Integration ‚Äî Three Readings, Two Wires
Skill
Students wire a BME280 (or similar I¬≤C environmental sensor) and use its Arduino library to read temperature, pressure, and humidity from a single two-wire connection. They compare this to their Semester 5 DHT11 ‚Äî same data, dramatically simpler wiring. Students log all three readings as CSV and pipe the output file directly into their Day 3 Python analysis script ‚Äî the first time two units within a semester feed each other deliberately. They write a 3-sentence analysis: what changed in the environment during the 10-minute log?

Day 10
DC Motor Control ‚Äî H-Bridge, PWM Speed & Bidirectional Drive
Skill
Students move from precise positioning (servo) to variable-speed bidirectional motion (DC motor via L298N H-bridge). They learn why motors cannot be driven directly from a microcontroller pin ‚Äî current requirements exceed what a GPIO can supply ‚Äî and how the H-bridge solves this. They write a sketch controlling speed via PWM analogWrite() and direction via logic pins. Students connect this to real applications: fans, conveyor belts, wheeled robots. A gear-reduction sketch in their notebook begins conceptually scoping the autonomous vehicle projects that appear in later semesters.

Day 12
Why One Interview Is Not Enough ‚Äî Research Validity & Planning
Intro
Teacher challenges students to reflect on their Semester 5 single interview: "How confident are you that one person's experience represents the problem broadly?" Introduction to research validity ‚Äî design decisions must be grounded in patterns across multiple people, not a single anecdote. Case studies of products that failed because they were designed for one person's preferences (often the designer's). Discussion: how many interviews before patterns emerge? (In HCD practice: approximately 5.) Students write a Research Plan ‚Äî target user group, 5 interview questions, schedule ‚Äî to be executed before Day 15. These HMW statements become the Semester 8 project brief.

Day 15
Affinity Mapping ‚Äî Synthesizing Multiple Interviews Into Design Themes
Skill
Students learn affinity mapping: each distinct observation, quote, or finding is written on a separate sticky note. Notes are grouped by emergent theme ‚Äî categories arise from the data, not from predetermined labels. Students identify 3‚Äì5 major themes and name them. From those themes, they write a refined set of "How Might We" statements ‚Äî more grounded and specific than Semester 5 because they are based on patterns across 3‚Äì5 real people. These HMW statements are formally submitted and become the brief for the Semester 8 integrated project. The stakes of this assignment are made explicit: the quality of your research determines the relevance of your design.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 8.2 CTE-TE 9.2 IAS-Math 9-10.S
Affinity mapping is qualitative data synthesis ‚Äî organizing unstructured observations into meaningful patterns. IAS-Math 9-10.S extends to qualitative research: finding clusters and themes in interview data is pattern recognition, a core statistical thinking skill applied beyond numerical contexts.
