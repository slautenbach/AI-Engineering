Day 1
Beyond Extrude ‚Äî Why Complex Geometry Needs New Tools
Intro
Students examine real-world products ‚Äî car fenders, ergonomic grips, medical device housings, consumer electronics ‚Äî and discuss why none of them can be modeled purely with extrudes and revolves. Teacher introduces the concept of swept and lofted geometry. Students sketch 5 real objects in their notebooks and annotate which surfaces would require Loft or Sweep. This sets purposeful motivation before learning the tools.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1 CTE-TE 6.3
CTE-TE 6.3: Apply advanced modeling and simulation tools to analyze and optimize designs. This standard anchors all advanced SolidWorks work this semester, including simulation in Unit 2.

Day 2
Sweep ‚Äî Extruding a Profile Along a Path
Skill
Students learn the Sweep feature: define a 2D profile sketch and a separate 3D path sketch, then sweep the profile along the path. Exercises include: a curved pipe fitting, a hand-grip profile swept along an ergonomic arc, and a decorative molding sweep. Students discover the relationship between path complexity and model stability ‚Äî a key constraint in advanced modeling. Notebook: sketches of all three profiles and paths, annotated with the feature used.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1 CTE-TE 6.3 IAS-Math 9-10.G
IAS-Math 9-10.G (Geometry): Apply geometric concepts in modeling situations. Sweep is the computational realization of a geometric solid of revolution along a non-linear axis ‚Äî a direct high school geometry connection.

Day 3
Loft ‚Äî Blending Between Profiles
Skill
Students learn the Loft feature: define two or more profile sketches on parallel planes and blend between them smoothly. Exercises: a square-to-circle transition (common in ducting), a tapered phone body profile, and a 3-profile wing cross-section. Introduction to guide curves to control the loft shape between profiles. Students compare hand-sketched approximations of each part to the CAD output ‚Äî reinforcing that CAD enables geometry impossible to draw precisely by hand.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1 CTE-TE 6.3 IAS-Math 9-10.G
Loft between profiles is a direct application of cross-sectional geometry (IAS-Math 9-10.G) ‚Äî the same concept as relating cross-sections of 3D solids to 2D shapes. Students see how math abstractions become engineering tools.
Assessment (Informal)

Day 4
Shell, Rib & Draft ‚Äî Making Parts Manufacturable
Skill
Students learn three fabrication-focused features: Shell (hollow out a solid to a uniform wall thickness ‚Äî critical for injection molding and 3D printing), Rib (add structural reinforcement fins inside a shell), and Draft (angle walls slightly so a part can be removed from a mold). These features connect directly to the manufacturing context students will encounter in the capstone ‚Äî real product design always considers how a part will be made, not just what it looks like.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 6.1 CTE-TE 6.2 CTE-TE 6.3
CTE-TE 6.2: Apply manufacturing and fabrication processes ‚Äî Shell, Rib, and Draft are design-for-manufacture features that directly address how geometry affects production feasibility. Students begin thinking like manufacturing engineers, not just designers.
Assessment (Informal)
Shell wall thickness uniform
Rib connects correctly
Draft angle applied

Advanced Modeling Project ‚Äî Design an Ergonomic Hand Tool
Project
Students design an ergonomic hand tool of their choice (screwdriver handle, stylus grip, garden trowel handle, etc.) using at least one Sweep or Loft feature plus Shell. The design must demonstrate consideration for the human hand ‚Äî introducing ergonomics as a design constraint for the first time. Students trace their own hand on paper, identify grip points, and use these measurements to inform their CAD geometry. EDP documentation required. Drawing package produced. Part 3D printed and measured against CAD.

Day 7
What is Simulation? ‚Äî Virtual Testing Before Physical Prototyping
Intro
Teacher introduces Finite Element Analysis (FEA) ‚Äî the computational method that divides a model into thousands of small elements and calculates how stress, strain, and deformation spread through a part under load. Students view industry examples: NASA spacecraft stress maps, car crash simulations, bridge load analysis. Key message: simulation saves time and money by finding failures digitally before building expensive prototypes. Discussion: how does this connect to the EDP's "test" step?
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.2 CTE-TE 6.3 IAS-Sci 9-10.PS
IAS-Sci 9-10.PS (Physical Science): Analyze forces, stress, and material behavior. FEA simulation directly visualizes these physics concepts ‚Äî stress concentration, deformation, and factor of safety are all grounded in 9th/10th grade science.
Vocabulary
FEA stress strain factor of safety mesh fixture load von Mises stress
Day 8
Running a SolidWorks Simulation ‚Äî Load, Fixture & Mesh
Skill
Students open SolidWorks Simulation (or SimulationXpress for student licenses) and run their first static analysis on a simple bracket. Step by step: assign material, define fixtures (where the part is held), apply loads (force in Newtons), mesh the part, run the solver, and interpret the color-coded stress plot. Students identify the highest-stress location and record the factor of safety. They then apply a fillet to the high-stress region and re-run ‚Äî observing how geometry changes the result.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.2 CTE-TE 6.3 IAS-Sci 9-10.PS IAS-Math 9-10.A
IAS-Math 9-10.A (Algebra): Factor of safety = material strength √∑ applied stress ‚Äî students perform this calculation and interpret its engineering meaning. Units, significant figures, and order-of-magnitude reasoning are reinforced.
Assessment (Informal)
Material assigned correctly
Simulation runs without errors
Factor of safety calculated
Fillet improvement documented
Day 9
Simulate Your Hand Tool ‚Äî Stress-Test the Ergonomic Design
Assess
Students run a SolidWorks Simulation study on their Unit 1 hand tool design ‚Äî applying a realistic grip force load (approximately 50N) and identifying any weak points. They must: record the maximum von Mises stress, calculate the factor of safety, identify the failure location on a screenshot, and propose one geometry change to improve it. Results are documented in their notebook and added to the portfolio as a "Simulation Report" ‚Äî a professional deliverable format they'll use through the capstone.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.2 CTE-TE 6.3 CTE-TE 8.1 IAS-Sci 9-10.PS IAS-Math 9-10.A
Formal Simulation Report Rubric
Simulation setup correct

Day 10
Sensor Ecosystem ‚Äî Temperature, Humidity, Ultrasonic & IR
Intro
Students receive an expanded sensor kit and are introduced to 4 new sensor types: DHT11/22 (temperature + humidity), HC-SR04 (ultrasonic distance), IR receiver, and a soil moisture sensor. For each, they examine the datasheet, identify the communication protocol (digital, analog, or single-wire), and wire + test a basic sketch. The lesson emphasizes reading datasheets ‚Äî a critical professional skill. Students build a "Sensor Reference Card" in their notebooks with pin diagram, sample code snippet, and typical use cases for each sensor.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 7.1 IAS-CS 8.AP.1 IAS-Sci 9-10.PS
Reading and applying a datasheet is an authentic engineering literacy task (CTE-TE 7.1). Each sensor type connects to a different physics phenomenon (IAS-Sci 9-10.PS): sound waves (ultrasonic), electromagnetic spectrum (IR), thermal energy (temperature).
Sensor Reference Card
Each entry includes: sensor name, pins (VCC/GND/Signal), wiring diagram, sample sketch, and 2 real-world use cases. This card is used throughout the rest of the semester and becomes a portfolio artifact.
Day 11
Multi-Sensor Sketches ‚Äî Reading Two Sensors Simultaneously
Skill
Students wire two sensors simultaneously and write a sketch that reads both in the loop() and makes a decision based on combined data (e.g., if temperature is high AND distance is close, blink red LED; otherwise blink green). This requires structuring the loop carefully to avoid blocking delays ‚Äî introduction to millis()-based timing as a non-blocking alternative to delay(). Students write a flowchart of their logic in their notebook before writing any code, practicing algorithm design before implementation.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 7.1 IAS-CS 8.AP.1 IAS-CS 8.AP.2 IAS-CS 9-10.AP.1
IAS-CS 9-10.AP.1: Design algorithms to solve complex problems. The multi-sensor logic with non-blocking timing is a genuinely complex algorithm ‚Äî writing the flowchart first fulfills this standard's design-before-code emphasis.
Assessment (Informal)
Flowchart written before coding
Both sensors read correctly
millis() timing used correctly
Day 12
Serial Data Logging ‚Äî Capturing Sensor Data Over Time
Skill
Students learn to output structured CSV-format data via Serial.print() (timestamp, sensor1, sensor2) and capture it to a text file using the Arduino IDE Serial Monitor or a simple Python logging script. They then import the CSV into a spreadsheet and create a line graph of the data over time. This is the first time students collect real-world sensor data, visualize it, and draw a conclusion from it ‚Äî the foundation of data-driven engineering decisions and a preview of the capstone's user research data workflow.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 7.1 IAS-CS 9-10.AP.1 IAS-Math 9-10.S
IAS-Math 9-10.S (Statistics): Represent and interpret data. Logging, graphing, and interpreting sensor time-series data directly fulfills this standard ‚Äî students calculate min, max, and average from their recorded dataset.

Day 15
What is Human-Centered Design? ‚Äî People Before Products
Intro
Teacher introduces human-centered design (HCD) as a design philosophy: start with the human experience, not the technology. Case studies: the redesign of a hospital patient room, the evolution of the iPhone keyboard, the OXO Good Grips peeler designed for people with arthritis. Key insight: the best engineers are deeply curious about people. Students discuss: "Think of a product that frustrated you. What did the designers not understand about how you actually use it?" This becomes their first HCD notebook entry.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 8.1 CTE-TE 8.2 CTE-TE 3.1
CTE-TE 8.2 (NEW): Evaluate the social, cultural, and ethical impacts of designed products and systems. HCD is the primary lens through which students will assess these impacts ‚Äî design decisions always affect real people.
Vocabulary
human-centered design empathy user stakeholder pain point insight design brief
Day 16
The User Interview ‚Äî How to Ask Questions That Reveal Real Problems
Skill
Students learn the core skill of user interviewing: asking open-ended questions, avoiding leading questions, listening for emotions and workarounds, and probing with "why" and "tell me more." Teacher models a bad interview vs. a good interview on video or live demo. Students practice in triads (interviewer, interviewee, observer) using the prompt: "Tell me about a daily task you find frustrating or difficult." Observer gives structured feedback using the interview checklist. This skill will be used directly in the capstone's user research phase.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 8.2 CTE-TE 4.1 CTE-TE 9.1 IAS-ELA 9-10.SL
CTE-TE 9.1 (NEW): Apply interpersonal and teamwork skills in engineering contexts. User interviewing is a professional interpersonal skill ‚Äî listening actively, responding without judgment, and probing for depth. IAS-ELA 9-10.SL: Initiate and participate in collaborative discussions.
Interview Checklist (Informal)

Day 17
Empathy Mapping ‚Äî Turning Interviews Into Insights
Skill
Students learn the Empathy Map framework: a tool for organizing interview data into 4 quadrants (Says, Thinks, Does, Feels). Working from their Day 16 interview notes, each student builds a full empathy map for their interview subject. Then, as a class, students look for patterns across multiple maps and practice writing "How Might We" (HMW) statements ‚Äî the bridge between observations and design opportunities. Two or three strong HMW statements are the deliverable for the capstone pre-work starting next semester.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 8.1 CTE-TE 8.2 CTE-TE 9.1 CTE-TE 3.1
Empathy mapping synthesizes human observation into actionable design direction ‚Äî the link between CTE-TE 8.2 (social/cultural impact) and CTE-TE 3.1 (design process). HMW statements are the output of this synthesis and a direct input to the EDP's "Define the Problem" step.

Day 18
HCD Field Assignment ‚Äî Interview Someone Outside of Class
Field
Students are assigned a real-world interview task: conduct a 10‚Äì15 minute user interview with someone outside of school (family member, neighbor, community member, or school staff) about a daily task or challenge in their life. Students use the interview framework from Day 16, take structured notes, and build an empathy map from the conversation. Results are shared in a brief "Insights Presentation" (2 min, Day 19 warm-up) and stored in their portfolio as the first piece of genuine user research ‚Äî a direct seed for the Sophomore capstone.
