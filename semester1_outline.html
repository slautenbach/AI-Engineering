The Engineering Design Process
Days 5‚Äì10 ¬∑ ~6 class sessions ¬∑ Core Skill + Mini-Project
‚åÑ
Day 5
Introducing the EDP ‚Äî The 8-Step Framework
Intro
Teacher introduces the Engineering Design Process: Define the Problem ‚Üí Research ‚Üí Brainstorm ‚Üí Select Solution ‚Üí Prototype ‚Üí Test ‚Üí Evaluate ‚Üí Communicate. Students annotate a visual of the EDP and discuss: "Why is it a cycle, not a line?" Real-world case study: How NASA iterated on a Mars rover wheel design.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 3.2
CTE-TE 3.1: Apply the design process to solve a problem. CTE-TE 3.2: Use modeling, prototyping, and testing in the design process. These two standards are the backbone of this entire unit.
Notebook Entry
Students diagram the EDP with their own labels and one personal example of a time they "iterated" on something in real life (sports, art, cooking, games).
Day 6
Defining Problems ‚Äî Constraints & Criteria
Skill
Deep dive into Step 1: Define the Problem. Students practice writing problem statements using the format "Design a [solution] that [achieves criteria] within [constraints]." They work in pairs to evaluate poorly written problem statements and rewrite them. Emphasis on specificity, measurability, and user focus.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 IAS-Math 7.EE
IAS-Math 7.EE: Solve real-world problems using equations and inequalities. Constraints are often mathematical ‚Äî students must express them numerically (max weight, max cost, min height).
Assessment (Informal)
Problem statement clarity
Constraints are measurable
Day 7
Brainstorming Techniques ‚Äî Beyond the First Idea
Skill
Students learn and practice structured brainstorming: SCAMPER, mind mapping, and the "crazy 8s" sketch method. A key rule is introduced: all ideas are valid during brainstorming. Students practice on a fun prompt ("redesign a school backpack for a student with limited hand mobility") and fill one full notebook page with sketched ideas.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 3.3
CTE-TE 3.3: Apply creative and critical thinking strategies to engineering problems. SCAMPER and Crazy 8s directly develop creative thinking as a systematic skill.
Notebook Entry
Full-page brainstorm using at least 2 methods. Minimum 8 distinct ideas. Teacher stamps/checks for completion.
Day 8
Mini-Challenge Launch ‚Äî The Egg Drop Problem
Project
Students receive the classic egg drop challenge with a twist: it must be completed entirely following the EDP documentation format in their notebook. They define the problem, list constraints (limited materials provided), brainstorm solutions, and select a design ‚Äî all documented before building begins. Groups of 2‚Äì3.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 3.2 IAS-Sci 7.PS
IAS-Sci 7.PS (Physical Science): Analyze and interpret data on properties of matter. Students must understand energy transfer and force to protect the egg ‚Äî connecting science content to engineering.
Assessment (Formal ‚Äî begins Day 9)
Problem statement written
Brainstorm documented
Selection rationale written
Day 9
Build & Test Day ‚Äî Egg Drop
Project
Students build prototypes from their selected design. Teacher circulates to prompt documentation. After a drop test, students record results, identify failures, and complete an "Evaluate & Iterate" section in their notebooks ‚Äî whether or not their egg survived. The iteration process is as important as the outcome.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.2 IAS-Sci 7.PS IAS-Sci 7.ETS
IAS-Sci 7.ETS (Engineering, Technology & Society): Define criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a successful solution. Students apply this standard directly in their test-evaluate loop.
Assessment (Formal)
Test results recorded accurately
Iteration plan written
Day 10
EDP Reflection & Notebook Check #1
Assess
Students write a structured reflection answering: (1) What step of the EDP was hardest? (2) What would you change about your design? (3) How is the EDP similar to something you do outside school? Teacher collects notebooks for first formal rubric assessment ‚Äî scored on organization, completeness, and depth of reflection.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 4.1 IAS-ELA 7.W
IAS-ELA 7.W (Writing): Write informative/explanatory texts to examine a topic. The reflection cross-maps to ELA writing standards, supporting interdisciplinary connections.
Formal Notebook Rubric

Sketches present & labeled

Day 11
Why Engineers Draw ‚Äî Intro to Technical Sketching
Intro
Students learn why hand sketching remains essential even in the age of CAD. Teacher demonstrates how engineers use quick sketches to communicate ideas before committing to digital models. Students practice basic line quality ‚Äî object lines, hidden lines, center lines, and dimension lines ‚Äî on dot grid paper.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2
CTE-TE 5.1: Use technical drawing standards and conventions. CTE-TE 5.2: Create and interpret technical drawings and models. These standards directly govern sketching and drawing throughout the 8-year pathway.
Materials
Dot grid paper, pencils (HB and 2H), ruler. No compass or protractor yet. Students label each line type on their practice sheet.
Day 12
Isometric Sketching ‚Äî Thinking in 3D
Skill
Introduction to isometric drawing using isometric dot paper. Students learn the 30-degree rule and practice drawing simple rectangular prisms, then combine shapes to form basic objects (steps, L-shapes, notched blocks). The focus is on seeing in 3D and transferring that vision to paper using consistent angles.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 IAS-Math 7.G
IAS-Math 7.G (Geometry): Solve problems involving 2D and 3D geometric shapes. Isometric drawing directly reinforces spatial geometry concepts ‚Äî drawing 3D shapes from 2D representations.
Assessment (Informal)
Consistent 30¬∞ angles
Shapes readable & proportional
Day 13
Isometric Practice ‚Äî Household Object Sketches
Skill
Students select 2 simple objects from a provided set (stapler, phone stand, small box, cup) and sketch each isometrically. Teacher circulates and provides direct instruction to individuals struggling with 3D visualization. Peer review using a provided checklist: consistent angles, proportional faces, visible edges.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.2 IAS-Math 7.G
Students practice spatial reasoning (IAS-Math 7.G) while applying technical drawing conventions (CTE-TE 5.2). Peer review reinforces CTE-TE 4.1 communication skills.
Peer Checklist (Informal)
3D form is recognizable
Lines are clean, no smearing
Day 14
Intro to Orthographic Projection ‚Äî 3 Views
Intro
Students learn the concept of orthographic (multiview) projection: Front, Top, and Right Side views. Teacher models using a physical 3D object (a small block or printed model) and its 3-view drawing. Students practice identifying which view is which using printed reference sets before attempting to draw.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 IAS-Math 7.G
Orthographic projection is the professional standard for technical drawings (CTE-TE 5.1). Visualizing 3D to 2D conversion directly maps to IAS-Math 7.G spatial reasoning standards.
Vocabulary
front view top view right side view projection lines first-angle vs third-angle
Day 15
Orthographic Drawing Practice ‚Äî Block Problems
Skill
Students complete a worksheet of 6 progressively complex orthographic drawing problems (given isometric view ‚Üí draw 3 orthographic views). Emphasis on alignment, proportional spacing, and consistent line types. Hidden lines are introduced for features not visible from the current view.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 IAS-Math 7.G
Assessment (Informal)
Views correctly aligned
Hidden lines used correctly
All 3 views present

Sketching Formal Assessment ‚Äî Isometric & Orthographic

Formal individual sketching assessment. Students receive a physical model and must produce: (1) one isometric sketch, and (2) three-view orthographic drawing, properly labeled and lined. This is graded using the formal rubric below. Students also add both drawings to their Engineering Notebook.

CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 IAS-Math 7.G

Days 17‚Äì18 ¬∑ ~2 class sessions ¬∑ Synthesis + Presentation

Day 17
Design a Solution ‚Äî Full EDP + Sketching Applied
Project
Students receive a simple design prompt (e.g., "Design a better pencil holder for our classroom using only materials in the makerspace"). They must complete ALL EDP steps in their notebook AND produce both an isometric and orthographic sketch of their chosen solution. No building required ‚Äî this is a planning and communication exercise.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 3.2 CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 IAS-Math 7.G
This project synthesizes all four units: design process (3.1, 3.2), technical drawing (5.1, 5.2), and geometry (7.G). It serves as a preview of how these skills integrate in real engineering work.
Formal Capstone Rubric
Complete EDP documented
Isometric sketch quality
Orthographic sketch quality
Constraints clearly defined
Day 18
Share-Out + Semester Reflection + Preview of Semester 2
Assess
Students do a 2-minute "pitch" of their design to the class (building public speaking skills for the ultimate capstone). Peer feedback is given using a provided form. Teacher collects final notebooks for semester grading. Class closes with a preview of Semester 2: "Next semester you'll take these same sketches and turn them into 3D models in Autodesk Inventor."
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 4.1 CTE-TE 4.2 IAS-ELA 7.SL
CTE-TE 4.2: Use presentation and communication technology effectively. IAS-ELA 7.SL (Speaking & Listening): Present claims and findings in a focused, coherent manner. The 2-minute pitch directly addresses both standards.
