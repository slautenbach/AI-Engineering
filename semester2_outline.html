Day 1
What is CAD? ‚Äî From Drafting Board to Software
Intro
Students connect their hand-drawing experience to digital CAD tools. Teacher walks through the history of drafting (T-squares ‚Üí AutoCAD ‚Üí parametric modeling) and explains what "parametric" means: dimensions drive geometry, not the other way around. Students explore Inventor's interface: Browser Bar, ViewCube, sketch environment, and ribbon tabs. No drawing yet ‚Äî just navigation and vocabulary.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1
CTE-TE 6.1: Select and use digital tools and technologies to design and model solutions. This standard anchors all CAD work for the rest of the pathway. Students begin connecting Sem 1 drawing standards (5.1, 5.2) to digital equivalents.
Vocabulary

Day 2
Inventor Interface Navigation ‚Äî Guided Tour
Skill
Teacher-led guided tutorial: students open a provided sample part file and practice orbiting, zooming, panning with mouse and ViewCube, switching between Home/Front/Top views, toggling wireframe vs. shaded display, and reading the model browser. They fill out a labeled interface screenshot in their notebook. Partner activity: one student navigates, one calls out the next action from a direction card.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 6.1 CTE-TE 5.2
Students interpret a technical model (CTE-TE 5.2) using digital tools (CTE-TE 6.1). The ability to read a CAD model corresponds directly to reading a hand drawing.

Day 3
2D Sketch Fundamentals ‚Äî Lines, Constraints & Dimensions
Skill
Students create their first 2D sketch in Inventor: a simple L-shaped profile. They practice using the Line tool, applying geometric constraints (horizontal, vertical, coincident), and adding parametric dimensions with the Smart Dimension tool. Key concept: a "fully constrained" sketch turns black ‚Äî students learn why this matters before extruding. They save their file using a teacher-provided naming convention.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 6.1 IAS-Math 7.G
IAS-Math 7.G: Drawing and describing geometric figures. Parametric dimensions in CAD directly reinforce geometric measurement and proportional reasoning from Grade 7 math.
Assessment (Informal)

Day 4
Extrude ‚Äî Giving Sketches a Third Dimension
Skill
Students learn the Extrude feature: taking a closed 2D sketch and adding depth. They model a simple rectangular block, then modify the extrusion height and observe how the model updates ‚Äî the core power of parametric CAD. Teacher introduces the concept of the Feature Tree (Browser Bar) and how features stack. Students model 3 progressive exercises: flat plate, stepped block, and T-profile.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1 IAS-Math 7.G
Extruding a 2D sketch into 3D directly connects to IAS-Math 7.G (relating 2D shapes to 3D solids ‚Äî cross-sections and volume). Students also fulfill CTE-TE 5.2 by creating technical 3D models.
Assessment (Informal)
All 3 exercises completed
Dimensions accurate
Day 5
Cut & Hole Features ‚Äî Removing Material
Skill
Students learn to sketch on the face of an existing solid and use Extrude (Cut) to remove material. They practice creating slots, pockets, and holes. Introduction to the Hole feature tool for standard-diameter holes. Students model a block with a centered rectangular pocket and two through-holes, then compare their model to a provided orthographic drawing ‚Äî reinforcing Sem 1 drawing interpretation skills.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1
Reading the orthographic drawing to verify the model (CTE-TE 5.1 & 5.2) reinforces the connection between hand drawing and CAD. Students interpret technical documentation to check their own work.
Assessment (Informal)
Pocket dimensions correct
Model matches ortho drawing
Day 6
Fillet, Chamfer & Revolve ‚Äî Refining Parts
Skill
Students learn edge modification features (Fillet and Chamfer) for rounding and beveling edges ‚Äî critical for 3D printability and real-world manufacturing. Introduction to the Revolve feature: spinning a 2D profile around an axis to create cylindrical geometry. Students model a simple cylindrical pin and a cup-shaped object using Revolve. Notebook entry: sketch the profile used for each Revolve feature and annotate the axis of revolution.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 6.1 IAS-Math 7.G IAS-Math 8.G
IAS-Math 8.G: Understand congruence and similarity using physical models. Revolving a 2D profile to generate a 3D shape is a direct application of rotational geometry ‚Äî a concept bridging 7th and 8th grade math.
Assessment (Informal)
Fillet/chamfer applied correctly
Revolve produces correct shape
Notebook profile sketched
Day 7
Redraw Your Capstone Sketch in CAD
Project
Students retrieve their Day 17 hand sketch from Semester 1 (the pencil holder design). They re-examine their orthographic views and begin building the part in Inventor, using their sketch as a blueprint. Teacher circulates and coaches on feature selection. This is the first time students experience the full Sketch ‚Üí CAD ‚Üí Physical pipeline. If the original design is not manufacturable, students iterate their sketch first ‚Äî documenting the change in their notebook.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1
Translating a hand sketch to a CAD model integrates the design process (3.1), technical drawing interpretation (5.2), and digital tool application (6.1) ‚Äî a true cross-unit synthesis moment.
Notebook Check (Informal)

Day 8
Capstone CAD ‚Äî Finish & Refine the Part
Project
Students complete their CAD model from Day 7. Teacher introduces appearance/material settings (cosmetic only at this stage) so students can visualize their part more realistically. Students also learn to measure their model using Inventor's Measure tool and verify it matches their intended dimensions. Peer review: partner checks model against the original orthographic sketch using a provided checklist.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.2 CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1
CTE-TE 3.2: Use modeling to represent a design solution. The CAD model is itself a prototype ‚Äî students are fulfilling this standard by creating a digital model of their design before physical fabrication.

Day 9
Formal Part Modeling Assessment
Assess
Students receive a new orthographic drawing (not their own design) and must produce a completed, accurate CAD part in Inventor from scratch in one class period. The drawing specifies all dimensions, features, and a feature list (e.g., "1 extrude, 1 cut, 2 fillets"). Students are graded on accuracy, feature tree organization, and file management. No peer collaboration during this assessment.

Day 10
Inventor Drawing Mode ‚Äî Auto-Generating Orthographic Views
Intro
Students learn Inventor's Drawing (.idw) environment. They open their capstone CAD part and generate a drawing sheet with Front, Top, and Right Side views using the Base View and Projected View tools. They see the direct connection to their Semester 1 hand drawings. Teacher introduces the standard drawing sheet format (title block, border, scale, material). Students complete a drawing of their capstone part and annotate it with their name, date, scale, and part name.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 CTE-TE 6.1
Students fulfill CTE-TE 5.1 (drawing standards and conventions) digitally for the first time. Title blocks, scale notation, and view placement match professional engineering drawing practice.
Assessment (Informal)

Day 11
Dimensioning a Drawing ‚Äî Smart Annotations
Skill
Students learn to add dimensions to their drawing views using Inventor's Dimension and Hole Note tools. Emphasis on: dimension placement conventions (no crossing dimension lines), when to dimension in each view, and how to notate hole diameters vs. depths. Students fully dimension their capstone drawing and compare it against their original hand-drawn orthographic from Semester 1 ‚Äî identifying improvements they would make to their original sketch.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 5.1 CTE-TE 5.2 IAS-Math 7.G
Dimension annotation directly applies geometric measurement standards (IAS-Math 7.G) in a professional context. Students also critique their own prior work ‚Äî a metacognitive learning moment.
Notebook Entry
Students write 3 sentences comparing their digital drawing to their Semester 1 hand sketch: what improved, what they would change, and what they now understand better about orthographic views.

Students also export a PDF of their drawing ‚Äî their first professional engineering document. This goes into their digital portfolio, which they will build throughout the 8-year program.

Day 13
How 3D Printing Works ‚Äî FDM, Slicing & Print Settings
Intro
Students learn Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM): how printers build parts layer by layer. Teacher introduces the slicing workflow: export STL from Inventor ‚Üí open in slicer software ‚Üí set layer height, infill, supports ‚Üí send to printer. Discussion: what design choices affect printability? (overhangs, wall thickness, hole orientation). Students evaluate 3 example parts and predict which would print poorly and why.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 6.1 CTE-TE 6.2 IAS-Sci 7.PS
CTE-TE 6.2: Apply manufacturing and fabrication processes to produce a designed product. FDM printing is the primary fabrication tool this semester. IAS-Sci 7.PS connects here through material properties (PLA thermoplastic behavior).
Vocabulary
FDM STL file layer height infill supports overhang wall thickness
Day 14
Design for Print ‚Äî Checking & Optimizing Your Model
Skill
Students review their capstone CAD part against a "Design for 3D Printing" checklist: minimum wall thickness (1.2mm), no overhangs >45¬∞ without supports, holes oriented for accuracy. They make any necessary modifications in Inventor and document changes in their notebook under "Print Iteration." Teacher slices each student's model as a class exercise to show estimated print time and highlight any issues.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.1 CTE-TE 3.2 CTE-TE 6.2
Modifying the design for manufacture is a direct application of CTE-TE 3.1 and 3.2 ‚Äî iterating based on real fabrication constraints. This mirrors professional design-for-manufacturing (DFM) practice.

Day 15
Print Day ‚Äî Submit Files & Monitor Prints
Project
Students export their STL, slice in slicer software, and submit print jobs. While prints run, students complete a "Print Prediction Sheet" in their notebook: estimated dimensions, expected challenges, and what they will check when the part comes off the printer. Students who finish early begin a second, more complex free-form part of their choice (teacher-approved) to print as a bonus.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 6.2 CTE-TE 3.2
Students fulfill CTE-TE 6.2 (fabrication) and CTE-TE 3.2 (modeling and prototyping) simultaneously. The physical artifact is the prototype predicted by their digital model ‚Äî a key conceptual milestone.

Day 16
Part Inspection ‚Äî Measuring & Evaluating the Print
Assess
Students receive their printed parts and inspect them using calipers or rulers. They complete a formal "Part Inspection Report" comparing measured dimensions to CAD model dimensions and record % error. They write a short analysis: What caused any deviations? What would they change in their model or print settings? This introduces the concept of manufacturing tolerances. Parts are kept for a future semester assembly project.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 3.2 CTE-TE 6.2 IAS-Math 7.RP
IAS-Math 7.RP (Ratios & Proportional Relationships): Computing % error between measured and nominal dimensions is a direct application of ratio and percent change from Grade 7 math.
Part Inspection Rubric (Formal)

% error calculated

Day 17
Portfolio Assembly ‚Äî Building Your First Engineering Portfolio Entry
Project
Students compile their first digital portfolio entry combining: (1) their original Sem 1 hand sketch, (2) their Inventor CAD model screenshot, (3) their professional drawing package PDF, (4) a photo of their printed part, and (5) their Part Inspection Report. Teacher introduces a simple portfolio template (Google Slides or PowerPoint) they will use and expand every semester. This artifact is the first page of what will become their senior capstone portfolio.
Indiana Standards
CTE-TE 4.1 CTE-TE 4.2 IAS-ELA 7.W
Portfolio creation addresses CTE-TE 4.1 & 4.2 (professional communication with technology) and IAS-ELA 7.W (writing to explain a process and its results).
Portfolio Checklist (Formal)

Captions explain each item
Layout is professional/clean

Next year, you'll learn more advanced CAD, add electronics, and start writing code to bring your designs to life."
